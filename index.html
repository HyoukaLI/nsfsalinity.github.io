A research project focused on leveraging sparse drifter trajectories for ocean salinity reconstruction.

Project Motivation

This project investigates ocean salinity imputation under sparse observational settings. The website is designed as a research-style landing page that summarizes the background, technical task, and current progress in a clean presentation format.

Background

Ocean Drifter: Pillars of Modern Marine Observation

1. Key Role: Autonomous platforms providing continuous, real-time data from inaccessible ocean regions (~71% Earth's surface).

2. Core Functions:

  • Monitor essential oceanographic variables (EOVs): Temperature, Salinity, Waves, Currents, Atmospheric-Ocean fluxes.
  • Support climate modeling, weather forecasting, and ecosystem studies.

3. Strategic Value:

  • Complement satellite/remote sensing by providing in-situ validation and subsurface measurements.
  • Enable long-term time-series data critical for detecting climate trends such as ocean warming and acidification.

Background

Ocean Drifter: Advantages and Challenges

  • Advantages
    • Direct observation: Drifters equipped with hydrological sensors can be used to measure salinity along the drifter track.
    • Suitable for complex nearshore environments: Salinity in offshore/coastal areas fluctuates dramatically. In these regions, satellite salinity data suffers from low resolution and high noise, whereas drifters can capture finer-grained dynamic variations.
  • Challenges
    • Data Sparsity: Insufficient capacity to capture long-range dependencies across sparse drifter tracks.
    • Instability or Bias: Heterogeneous data distributions caused by drifting sensors and dynamic ocean conditions.

Task 1

Introduction, problem definition, core idea, methodology, and results

Introduction

Why Ocean Salinity Imputation Matters

Ocean salinity is a fundamental variable for understanding ocean circulation, climate processes, water-mass formation, and nutrient transport. Nearshore and coastal waters are especially challenging because salinity changes dynamically under the influence of freshwater inflow, tides, wind forcing, and human activity.

Drifters equipped with hydrological sensors can measure salinity along their tracks, but the resulting observations are sparse, irregular, and noisy. This makes the task naturally an imputation problem: estimating missing salinity values across time and space from limited drifter observations.

Traditional methods such as remote sensing, optimal interpolation, and Kriging are limited by linearity assumptions, spatial stationarity, low revisit frequency, cloud occlusion, and weak capacity to model complex spatiotemporal dependencies.

Problem Definition

Spatiotemporal Salinity Imputation from Sparse Drifter Trajectories

The task is to reconstruct missing salinity values from sparse and irregularly sampled drifter trajectories over time and geographic grids.

In the paper, the drifter observations are represented as a partially observed tensor over time, latitude, longitude, and sensor variables, while a binary mask indicates which values are observed and which are missing.

The learning objective is to estimate salinity at arbitrary times and locations in the study area, despite severe sparsity, heterogeneous sensor distributions, and complex ocean dynamics.

Core Idea

The paper proposes OASIS, a diffusion adversarial framework for ocean salinity imputation. Its core idea is to combine three strengths into one unified system.

Normalization Reduces distribution shift and bias across drifting sensors.
Global Dependency Capturing Learns long-range spatiotemporal correlations using a transformer-based module.
Diffusion Adversarial Learning Progressively refines imputed salinity fields under severe sparsity.

A key design choice is conditioning the model on tidal height, which serves as a practical, low-cost proxy for otherwise unmeasured physical drivers.

Methodology

OASIS Pipeline

  • Step 1: Normalize drifter observations to reduce sensor-specific distribution shifts.
  • Step 2: Encode temporal and spatial information with a transformer-based global dependency module.
  • Step 3: Use a scheduler-guided diffusion adversarial network to generate and refine salinity imputations.
  • Step 4: Condition the model on tide information to improve physical consistency and predictive accuracy.

The generator reconstructs realistic salinity values, while the discriminator distinguishes real from generated samples after diffusion-based perturbation. Together, they improve both robustness and fidelity.

Results

Key Findings

  • OASIS consistently outperforms traditional and neural baselines across multiple benchmark datasets.
  • On the FP Observed dataset, it improves over the best baseline in MAE, RMSE, and MAPE.
  • It captures both large-scale salinity gradients and localized variations under sparse sampling.
  • Ablation studies show that normalization, global dependency capturing, and scheduler diffusion all contribute substantially.

The paper also shows that tide information becomes useful when integrated through the proposed framework, whereas naive inclusion in simpler baselines can provide only marginal or inconsistent benefits.

Current Progress

Method overview and visualization-based experimental results

Method Overview

GridGNN for Ocean Salinity Imputation

Our current progress focuses on GridGNN, a trajectory-aware graph learning framework designed for ocean salinity imputation from sparse drifter observations. The method first maps irregular drifter measurements onto a regular spatial grid, then constructs local graphs within each grid cell and a global graph across cells.

  • Grid Construction: Irregular drifter observations are discretized into latitude–longitude grid cells to provide a consistent spatial framework.
  • Trajectory-Aware Graph Construction: The model combines trajectory edges and spatial KNN edges, so it can capture both along-track continuity and local spatial similarity.
  • Local Message Passing: Two graph streams process trajectory relations and spatial neighborhood relations separately.
  • Multi-Graph Fusion: The two streams are fused into a unified local representation.
  • Global Propagation: Information is propagated across adjacent grid cells to recover larger-scale structures.
  • Prediction Head: Final node embeddings are mapped to salinity predictions for imputation.

This design allows the framework to model both fine-scale local variability and large-scale spatial coherence, which is especially important under sparse, irregular, and heterogeneous drifter sampling.

Experimental Results

Visualization-Based Findings

To qualitatively evaluate reconstruction quality, the paper visualizes predicted salinity distributions across nine geographically diverse regions. These regions cover multiple oceanographic regimes, including coastal shelves, equatorial oceans, transport-dominated regions, and high-latitude Arctic environments.

  • Coastal and Equatorial Regions: The model captures localized salinity transitions and mesoscale structures without excessive smoothing.
  • Transport-Dominated Regions: Reconstructed fields preserve coherent zonal gradients and along-trajectory advective consistency.
  • Sparsely Sampled Regions: Even under fragmented trajectories and low neighborhood density, the model still produces spatially consistent and physically plausible reconstructions.
  • Overall Observation: The visual agreement between observations and predictions shows that the model preserves both large-scale spatial structure and fine-scale variability.

These visualization results suggest that the model achieves not only low numerical error, but also strong spatial realism across very different marine environments.
