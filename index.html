Background

Ocean drifters are a key source for marine observation

Ocean drifters provide continuous in-situ observations over large and often difficult-to-access marine regions. They support monitoring tasks for salinity, temperature, waves, currents, and atmosphere–ocean interactions.

Compared with remote sensing products, drifters can better reflect local and nearshore variability, which is especially useful in dynamic coastal environments. However, their measurements remain sparse, irregular, and strongly affected by tides and currents.

Why this matters

  • Direct salinity observations along sparse trajectories
  • Strong potential for nearshore and complex-region monitoring
  • Supports dense reconstruction from irregular marine sensing
  • Motivates graph-based spatial and temporal modeling
Dataset

Observed and simulated drifter datasets

FP Observed (NSF Dataset)

  • Real-world drifter dataset collected by HBOI at Florida Atlantic University
  • After preprocessing, 3 valid deployment days are retained
  • Focus day: Jun 16, 2016 near Fort Pierce Inlet, Florida, USA
  • 4 trajectories with 11,426 salinity measurements

GoM Simulated

  • Synthetic drifter data generated from a numerical ocean current model
  • Subset extracted near the Mississippi River delta
  • 69 trajectories and 52,253 time steps in the studied subset
  • Organized into GoM-10, GoM-11, and GoM-12 monthly segments
Problem Definition

Sea surface salinity imputation as tensor completion

The task is formulated as a partially observed tensor reconstruction problem. Given sparse drifter trajectories with salinity and auxiliary variables, the goal is to recover a dense salinity field across time and space.

T Temporal steps
H × W Spatial grid over latitude and longitude
C Sensor and environmental variables
Method

Graph-aware ocean salinity imputation

Input Representation

Sparse drifter trajectories are organized into a partially observed spatio-temporal tensor with salinity and auxiliary measurements.

Local Graph

Captures local hydrological structure and short-range spatial dependencies to preserve fine-grained salinity variation.

Global Graph

Models long-range context such as tides, currents, and large-scale circulation with coarser regional dependencies.

Output

Produces a dense salinity field for imputation, reconstruction, and downstream oceanographic analysis.

Local modeling focus

  • Small-scale salinity gradient variation
  • Fine-grained local feature propagation
  • Retention of nearshore structural detail

Global modeling focus

  • Long-distance regional dependency
  • Tidal and circulation-driven influence
  • Lower computational cost at grid level

Deployment

Operational workflow

Step 1

Accept a single location input or batch location input.

Step 2

Query NOAA CO-OPS API to retrieve tide information associated with the input region.

Step 3

Run OASIS imputation and produce salinity reconstruction with visualization outputs.

Current Stage

Graph-aware ocean salinity imputation

Local graph branch

The current framework emphasizes preserving local salinity structure under sparse and irregular drifter observations.

Global graph branch

Regional context is integrated to better capture large-scale ocean dynamics and long-range environmental effects.

Future Work

Next directions

Datasets
